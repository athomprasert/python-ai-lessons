The improvement playbook mindset

L15 left you with a dashboard of two-dozen numbers. The danger: you stare at them, panic, change six things at once, and never know which change helped. Use this lesson as a reference and follow three rules:

  1. Change one thing per eval cycle. Otherwise the eval can't tell you which fix actually moved the needle.
  2. Cheapest fixes first. Threshold tuning beats a new model. New model beats re-architecting. Re-architecting beats fine-tuning.
  3. Watch for trade-offs. Many metrics oppose each other — improving one often degrades another. The right choice depends on your application.
The improvement loop
Run eval → identify worst-scoring metric → pick cheapest fix from playbook
       → apply ONE change → re-run eval → did it help?
                                            │
              ┌─── Yes ──→ keep, repeat ────┘
              │
              └─── No ───→ revert, try next fix

The trade-off map

Some metrics genuinely oppose each other. Knowing which is half the battle.

Precision     ←→     Recall
   "fewer, more relevant"     "more, possibly noisier"
   ↑ smaller K                ↑ larger K
   ↑ stricter threshold       ↑ multi-query, hybrid

Latency       ←→     Quality
   "faster"                   "better"
   ↑ smaller models           ↑ bigger models, more chunks
   ↑ caching, batching        ↑ reranker, multi-step pipelines

Cost          ←→     Quality
   "cheaper"                  "better"
   ↑ smaller models           ↑ frontier models
   ↑ prompt caching (L11)     ↑ more eval LLM-judges
   ↑ lower K                  ↑ higher K + reranker

Faithfulness  ←→     Helpfulness
   "won't answer if uncertain" "tries harder to give an answer"
   ↑ stricter refusal rules    ↑ more permissive prompts
   ↑ higher confidence cutoff  ↑ extrapolate-from-context allowed

None of these are pure wins or losses — they're positions on a continuum chosen by the application. A medical Q&A bot lives on the Faithfulness end; a creative-writing assistant lives on the Helpfulness end.

Why Precision and Recall oppose each other — a closer look

The first row above is the most important and the most counter-intuitive. Let me build the intuition properly.

The single math fact behind the trade-off

Both formulas share a numerator and differ only in the denominator:

                    relevant chunks in top K
   Precision@K  =   ────────────────────────
                              K

                    relevant chunks in top K
   Recall@K     =   ────────────────────────
                       total relevant chunks
                       (FIXED for the question)

Recall's denominator is fixed. It doesn't grow when K grows.
Precision's denominator IS K. It grows in lockstep with K.

That asymmetry is the whole story.

Concrete example — 10 chunks, 3 relevant

Pretend your corpus has just 10 chunks. The user asks one question. Three chunks are relevant — chunks #2, #5, #8. Your retriever scores all 10 and returns them in this order (best score first):

Position 1:  #2   ✓ relevant
Position 2:  #4   ✗
Position 3:  #5   ✓ relevant
Position 4:  #7   ✗
Position 5:  #8   ✓ relevant
Position 6:  #1   ✗
Position 7:  #3   ✗
Position 8:  #9   ✗
Position 9:  #6   ✗
Position 10: #10  ✗

Watch what happens to Precision and Recall as we slide K from 1 to 10:

                            relevant   Precision@K       Recall@K
                            in top K    = rel/K           = rel/3
   ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
   K=1   [✓]                    1       1/1 = 1.00       1/3 = 0.33
   K=3   [✓ ✗ ✓]                2       2/3 = 0.67       2/3 = 0.67
   K=5   [✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✓]            3       3/5 = 0.60  ↓    3/3 = 1.00 ← CEILING
   K=7   [✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✗]        3       3/7 = 0.43  ↓    3/3 = 1.00 (flat)
   K=10  [✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✗ ✗ ✗ ✗]  3       3/10= 0.30  ↓    3/3 = 1.00 (flat)

Plot those columns on a single axis as K grows:

Recall climbs and then plateaus at 1.0 once it's found all 3 relevant chunks. It can never go down — adding more chunks can only find more relevant ones, never lose them.

Precision peaks early and decays. Each new position past the relevant chunks adds 1 to the denominator without adding to the numerator. Pure dilution.

Why "oppose"?

It's not that they're enemies. They measure different sides of the same coin with different denominators:

                                      Of the K we returned…  → Precision
   You return K chunks.  ───→  Ask:
                                      Of the total relevant…  → Recall

When K grows:

  • Recall asks "did we find more of the limited relevant set?" → answer can only be yes or same
  • Precision asks "what fraction of what we returned was useful?" → answer falls every time you add an irrelevant chunk

So they don't truly oppose — they answer different questions about the same retrieval. The "opposition" is just that K is the dial, and turning it moves them in opposite directions.

Mental model — fishing nets

   SMALL NET (low K = 3)             BIG NET (high K = 10)

       ╭─────╮                       ╭─────────────────────╮
       │ ✓ ✗ │                       │ ✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✗ ✗ ✗ ✗ │
       │  ✓  │                       │                     │
       ╰─────╯                       ╰─────────────────────╯

   Caught: 2 fish, 1 stick           Caught: 3 fish, 7 sticks
   Almost everything I caught        I got all the fish, but
   was a fish (high precision)       look at all this junk
   But I missed one (low recall)     (low precision, high recall)

A small net is selective — you don't haul much, but most of what you do haul is what you wanted.
A big net is comprehensive — you don't miss fish, but you also drag up sticks.

Neither net is "better." A grocery shopper wants precision (small net — only good fruit). A research biologist wants recall (big net — every specimen). RAG sits in between, and the eval tells you where.

Direct vs indirect dials

The trade-off table at the top lists K as the direct dial, plus indirect ones. They all push the curve the same way:

  • Stricter threshold = "drop chunks below score X" = effectively a smaller net = precision-favouring
  • Multi-query / hybrid = "cast more nets in different ways" = bigger effective net = recall-favouring

When a fix improves both (the force multipliers)

K-tuning is a slide along the existing curve. Some upgrades shift the whole curve up:

  • Better embedding model → relevant chunks score higher → both metrics improve
  • Reranker → moves relevant chunks to earlier positions → Precision@K climbs without recall dropping
  • Contextual retrieval → chunks the system understands better → both improve

These are the "near-Pareto" wins from the force-multipliers section below. K-tuning is the cheap-but-zero-sum dial; force-multipliers are the upgrades that genuinely move the frontier.

Layer 1 — Per-query depth metrics

Hit@K low

"Sometimes the right chunk doesn't appear in top-K at all."

  1. Increase K — the cheapest fix. Often "we just needed K=10, not K=3."
  2. Switch to a stronger embedding model (e.g. gemini-embedding-2, or a domain-specific model) — moderate cost, often big gain.
  3. Add Contextual Retrieval (L11) — adds context blurbs so chunks know what document they live in.
  4. Add hybrid search (L12) — catches exact-ID queries the vector model smears together.
  5. Add a reranker on top of high-K retrieval — rerank top-50 down to top-5 with a cross-encoder.

Bigger K hurts Precision@K and increases generation cost (more tokens per prompt).

Precision@K low

"Half the chunks I return are irrelevant — wasting Gemini's context window."

  1. Decrease K — if recall stays acceptable, this is free.
  2. Add a similarity threshold (you already have one in rag_chroma.py — try raising it from 0.5 → 0.6 or 0.7).
  3. Better chunking (L8) — recursive or document-structure-aware chunkers give cleaner, more focused chunks.
  4. Reranker — the precision champion. Cross-encoders directly score "does this chunk answer this query?"

Lower K and stricter thresholds hurt Recall@K. Reranking adds latency.

Recall@K low

"Multi-step questions miss because we only return some of the relevant chunks."

  1. Increase K.
  2. Multi-query expansion — generate 3–5 paraphrased queries from the user's question, retrieve for each, union the results.
  3. Contextual Retrieval — fewer chunks get missed because of pronoun/reference problems.
  4. Semantic chunking — fewer "split mid-thought" chunks where the relevant info gets cut.
  5. Query decomposition — for complex questions, break into sub-queries and retrieve for each.

Multi-query expansion costs extra embedding calls. K↑ hurts precision.

MRR low

"Relevant chunks are in top-K, but rarely at rank 1."

  1. Reranker (cross-encoder) — the single biggest MRR boost in production. Hands-on below.
  2. HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings) — generate a fake "ideal answer" with an LLM, embed that, retrieve docs near the fake answer. The fake answer is closer to real chunks than the user's question is.
  3. Query rewriting — clean up vague user queries before retrieval.

Each adds an extra step → higher latency and cost.

Context Precision low

"Relevant chunks are in the list, but they're ranked behind irrelevant ones."

  1. Reranker — exactly what it's designed for.
  2. Threshold filter — drops weak matches before they pollute the rank.
  3. Tune RRF weights if hybrid (L12) — give whichever ranker is more trusted slightly more weight.

Context Recall low

"The chunks we return don't cover all the claims in the expected answer."

  1. Increase K.
  2. Hybrid search for diversity.
  3. Query decomposition — multi-claim answers often need multi-claim retrieval.
  4. Better chunking — chunks that span concepts cover more claims per chunk.

Context Entities Recall low

"Identifiers, error codes, and version numbers in the expected answer don't end up in retrieved chunks."

  1. Hybrid search (BM25) — the canonical fix. L12.
  2. Entity-aware chunking — keep IDs and their context in the same chunk.
  3. Metadata filtering — index entities as metadata so you can filter by them at query time.

Faithfulness low / Hallucination Rate high

"The LLM is making things up that aren't in the retrieved chunks."

  1. Stricter system prompt — explicit "do not use outside knowledge" + a refusal phrase.
  2. Lower temperature (e.g. 0.0–0.2) — less creative, more grounded.
  3. Refusal threshold — refuse to answer when retrieval scores are low.
  4. Fact-check pass — second LLM call: "is every claim supported by context?" If not, regenerate or refuse.
  5. Larger / better-aligned chat model — frontier models hallucinate less.

More refusals — Helpfulness drops. Fact-check pass = 2× cost & latency.

Groundedness low

"The answer is roughly faithful, but individual sentences aren't traceable to chunks."

  1. Sentence-level citation prompt — "every sentence MUST end with [N]."
  2. Claim-decomposition pass — break the answer into atomic claims; check each against context.
  3. Structured output (JSON: {claims: [{text, source_chunk}]}) — the model is forced to attach citations.

Citation Accuracy low

"Citations exist but they're wrong — sentence cites chunk 3 but the claim is from chunk 5."

  1. Per-sentence citation prompt with explicit chunk numbering — see L9's prompt.
  2. Cite-or-refuse rule — "if you can't cite, say I don't know."
  3. Post-hoc verification — for each citation, re-check with a small LLM call.

Verification adds N extra LLM calls per answer.

Answer Semantic Similarity low

"Answers are factually right but worded differently from the expected answer."

  1. Few-shot examples in the prompt — show the LLM the desired phrasing.
  2. Style guide in system instruction (e.g. "respond in 2-3 sentences, formal tone").
  3. Larger model if the small one is rambling.

Don't optimise this too hard — semantic similarity ≠ correctness. Use it as a signal, not a target.

Refusal Correctness low

"The system makes up answers when it should be saying 'I don't know'."

  1. Explicit refusal threshold — if best retrieval score < 0.5, refuse.
  2. Few-shot refusal examples in the prompt — show "I don't have that information" answers in the system instruction.
  3. Tool-use deferral — let the agent call a "search the web" tool when local retrieval fails (next lesson).
Layer 2 — Safety

Toxicity flagged

  1. Moderation API gate — pre-filter user queries and post-filter generated answers (Perspective API, OpenAI Moderation).
  2. System prompt safety rules — explicit "do not produce harmful, biased, or policy-violating content."
  3. Regenerate-on-fail — if a moderation check trips, regenerate or refuse.

PII leakage

  1. Pre-filter the corpus with Microsoft Presidio at indexing time — strip or tag PII before chunks are embedded.
  2. Output scanning — run Presidio on the answer too, redact what shouldn't pass.
  3. Authorisation-aware retrieval — filter chunks by the user's permissions via metadata where clauses.

Bias / fairness fail

  1. Demographic-aware system prompt — explicitly instruct neutrality on protected attributes.
  2. Audit the corpus — biased docs in → biased answers out.
  3. Demographic-swap eval in CI — fail the build if scores diverge across swaps.
Layer 3 — Operations

Latency high (especially p95/p99)

  1. Prompt caching (L11) — biggest win for repeated prefixes.
  2. Streaming responses — perceived latency drops to "time-to-first-token", not total time.
  3. Smaller chat model — Flash instead of Pro; quality often acceptable.
  4. Parallel retrieval calls — if you need vector + BM25 + reranker, run them concurrently.
  5. Lower K — fewer chunks → smaller prompt → faster generation.
  6. Model distillation (advanced) — fine-tune a small model on the big model's outputs.

Smaller models hurt quality metrics. Lower K hurts recall.

Cost per query high

  1. Prompt caching (L11) — 5–10× reduction on contextualisation indexing.
  2. Smaller models for sub-tasks — Flash or Nano for context-blurb generation, judges, query rewriting.
  3. Embedding-based routing — cheap classifier decides if RAG is even needed for this query.
  4. Lower K — eval-tuned, not gut-tuned.
  5. Cache common answers — semantic cache (cosine match against past Q&As) skips the whole pipeline.

Eval Set Coverage low

  1. Sample real queries from logs — add the most-uncovered ones to your eval.
  2. Cluster failed queries (L15 failure clustering) — each cluster suggests new eval categories.
  3. Aim for >80% coverage — measure as % of real queries within similarity threshold of any eval question.
Layer 4 — System health

Drift detected (eval scores degrading over time)

  1. Pin model versions (e.g. gemini-3.5-flash-001 instead of gemini-3.5-flash) — vendor updates can silently change behaviour.
  2. Re-index on corpus change with content-hash IDs (L10).
  3. Schedule nightly eval runs; alert on regressions > threshold.

Robustness low (paraphrases produce different answers)

  1. Query rewriting layer — normalise phrasing before retrieval.
  2. Paraphrase augmentation at indexing — generate 2–3 alternate phrasings of each chunk's first sentence and store in metadata.
  3. Multi-query expansion at query time — already mentioned for recall, also helps robustness.

The four force-multiplier upgrades

A handful of techniques move multiple metrics at once. If you only learn four upgrades beyond what you've already built, learn these.

1. Cross-encoder reranking

Retrieve top-50 with vector / hybrid (cheap, recall-oriented), then have a cross-encoder directly score "does this chunk answer this query?" — and keep the top 5.

The trick: cross-encoders see the query and the chunk together (not as separate vectors), so they understand fine-grained relevance. Slower per-pair, but you only run them on 50 candidates, not the whole corpus.

Boosts: Precision@K, MRR, Context Precision, often Faithfulness too (cleaner context = less hallucination).

from sentence_transformers import CrossEncoder

reranker = CrossEncoder("cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-12-v2")

def rerank(question, candidate_chunks, top_n=5):
    # Build (query, chunk) pairs and score them all in one batched call
    pairs = [(question, c) for c in candidate_chunks]
    scores = reranker.predict(pairs)

    # Sort chunks by score, keep top_n
    ranked = sorted(zip(scores, candidate_chunks), reverse=True)
    return [(float(s), c) for s, c in ranked[:top_n]]
New library: sentence-transformers
The standard Python package for embedding and reranking models that aren't behind an API. CrossEncoder wraps a Hugging Face model that scores (query, chunk) pairs. ms-marco-MiniLM-L-12-v2 is a small, fast, well-tuned reranker — runs locally on CPU in ~10ms per pair. Install: pip install sentence-transformers. Reference

Wire it into hybrid_search as a final stage:

def hybrid_with_rerank(q, coll, bm25, chunk_ids, top_n=5):
    # Retrieve a wide set first — recall-oriented
    candidates = hybrid_search(q, coll, bm25, chunk_ids, k_each=25, top_k=25)
    chunks = [c for _, c in candidates]
    # Rerank → narrow to precise top_n
    return rerank(q, chunks, top_n=top_n)

2. HyDE — Hypothetical Document Embeddings

Ask the LLM to generate a fake answer to the question, then embed that fake answer and retrieve docs near it. Counter-intuitive but it works: the fake answer's vocabulary is closer to real chunks than the question's vocabulary is.

Boosts: Recall@K, Hit@K, especially on short or vague queries.

def hyde_search(question, collection):
    fake_answer = client.models.generate_content(
        model="gemini-3.5-flash",
        contents=f"Write a short hypothetical answer (2 sentences) to this question. "
                 f"It does not need to be factually correct.\n\nQ: {question}",
    ).text
    q_vec = embed(fake_answer, "RETRIEVAL_QUERY")
    return collection.query(query_embeddings=[q_vec], n_results=TOP_K)

Trade-off: +1 LLM call per query → latency & cost. Best for queries where retrieval recall is your biggest pain.

3. Multi-query expansion

Generate 3–5 paraphrased variants of the user's question, retrieve for each, union (or RRF-fuse) the results.

Boosts: Recall@K, Context Recall, Robustness (same answer for differently-phrased questions).

def expand_query(question, n=3):
    prompt = f"""Generate {n} alternative phrasings of this question for retrieval.
Return ONLY a JSON list of strings, no explanation.
Question: {question}"""
    response = client.models.generate_content(
        model="gemini-3.5-flash", contents=prompt
    )
    return [question] + json.loads(response.text)

def multi_query_search(question, collection):
    queries = expand_query(question)
    all_results = []
    for q in queries:
        q_vec = embed(q, "RETRIEVAL_QUERY")
        r = collection.query(query_embeddings=[q_vec], n_results=TOP_K)
        all_results.append(r["ids"][0])
    # Fuse the lists with RRF (you have rrf_fuse from L12)
    return rrf_fuse(*all_results)
New syntax: argument unpacking with *
rrf_fuse(*all_results) spreads the list into individual arguments. If all_results = [list1, list2, list3], this becomes rrf_fuse(list1, list2, list3). The opposite of *args on the receiving side (L15). Useful when your function accepts a variable number of inputs.

4. Hybrid search (already in L12)

Vector + BM25 + RRF — covers vector's blind spot on identifiers. Boosts: Hit@K, Context Entities Recall, often Precision too.

Already in your rag_chroma.py — but re-stated here because it's a force multiplier worth remembering when ordering fixes.

Lowest-effort improvement. You already have THRESHOLD = 0.5; raise it.

# Before

When to stop chasing metrics

Diminishing returns

Past a point, each improvement costs more than it returns:

  • If the gap between your best metric run and human-expert performance is <5%, your bottleneck is probably the eval set, not the system.
  • If a fix improves a metric by <2 percentage points and adds latency or cost, don't ship it.
  • If two fixes contradict each other (e.g. "lower K for cost" vs "higher K for recall"), make the trade-off per use case, not per system.

Production teams set SLO floors per metric — "Faithfulness must stay > 0.90, latency p95 must stay < 4s, then maximise recall" — and stop tuning when all floors are met.

Quick check

Q1. Your eval shows Recall@5 = 0.55 (low) and Precision@5 = 0.85 (high). What's the cheapest first fix?

  • Increase K — you're being too restrictive; you can afford some precision loss for recall gain
  • Add a reranker
  • Switch embedding models
  • Decrease K

Q2. Why does cross-encoder reranking improve Precision@K and MRR more than swapping to a better bi-encoder?

  • Cross-encoders use more parameters
  • Cross-encoders are open-source
  • Cross-encoders see the query and chunk together — they can model fine-grained relevance that bi-encoders (which embed query and chunk separately) can't
  • Cross-encoders have larger context windows

Q3. HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings) helps because…

  • It's faster than regular vector search
  • It eliminates the need for chunking
  • It's required by the Gemini API
  • A fake answer's vocabulary is closer to real chunks than the user's question is, so retrieval finds better matches

Q4. Faithfulness is 0.78 (you want > 0.95). The cheapest first fix is…

  • Switch to a larger chat model
  • Add stricter system prompt rules + lower temperature
  • Re-architect with a fact-check pass
  • Migrate to a different vector DB

Q5. Why "change one thing per eval cycle"?

  • Larger changes break CI
  • It's required by RAGAS
  • If you change two things at once and scores move, you can't tell which change caused it — so the eval can't guide future decisions
  • It's a Python convention

Q6. Your eval shows reranking gives +0.18 MRR but adds 250ms p95 latency. When is this NOT worth shipping?

  • When your latency SLO is already at its limit and 250ms would breach it
  • Always — never ship reranking
  • When your MRR is already 0.70
  • Only on weekends
