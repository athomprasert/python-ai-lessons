Discovery & positioning kit — MuleSoft in the Agentic Enterprise

A working session structured around one objective: capture the customer's as-is IT landscape, co-define a to-be architecture, position MuleSoft into it, and leave with the artifacts that earn the technical win. Two canvases (as-is, to-be), a gap in the middle, and a win checklist at the end.

The objective, stated plainly
You are not here to pitch a diagram. You are here to (1) capture what they have, (2) agree what "good" looks like, (3) show MuleSoft as the shortest credible path from one to the other, and (4) walk out with proof the technical evaluator is convinced. The architecture draw is the middle third — the as-is capture and the win criteria are what actually move the deal.
Before you quote product specifics
Agent Fabric, Omni Gateway, MCP Bridge, and the MCP/A2A connectors are new and fast-moving (several GA'd in 2026). Component roles and the architecture shape are stable and safe to whiteboard. Exact feature lists, GA dates, and dialog details drift — verify current specifics before making commitments. If asked something you're unsure of, capture it as a follow-up rather than guessing; following up is a trust-builder in a technical evaluation. See the agentic reference architecture doc for the verified detail behind each box.

The session arc (60 min)

Keep the order: as-is before to-be, gap before pitch. You can't position MuleSoft credibly until their current landscape is on the wall.

0–5Frame & permission. "I want to map your current landscape, then sketch where it's heading — together, on the wall. Sound good?" Get the pen.
5–25Capture the as-is. Draw their current landscape — systems, integration, API estate, existing AI/agent work, ownership, pains. This is the biggest block for a reason.
25–40Co-define the to-be. Overlay the agentic fabric onto the as-is boxes — box by box, each answering a pain they named.
40–50Name the gap & the path. As-is vs to-be side by side; the incremental transition (smallest first win).
50–58Test for the win. Surface decision criteria, who signs off, what proof they need. Listen for red flags.
58–60Capture & commit. Photo both canvases, agree the next artifact (PoC scope, deep-dive, or a gap to verify).

Pre-session prep checklist

  • Do they already own Anypoint or a competitor's integration platform? (Shapes whether this is "extend" or "displace".)
  • What agent work is already happening — Agentforce? A data-science team with LangGraph? A copilot pilot?
  • Who's in the room — and crucially, who is the technical decision-maker / evaluator you need to win?
  • Their messiest backends (SAP, mainframe, legacy SOAP)? Strongest "why integration matters" anchors.
  • Any stated AI mandate from leadership (cost, risk, "agentic transformation")? Tie the to-be to it.
  • What would a competitor say here, and where does MuleSoft genuinely win vs merely tie?
  • Re-skim the reference architecture + MCP positioning docs.
  • Markers. Photo permission. Two canvases planned (as-is on the left, to-be on the right).

Canvas 1 — capture the as-is (draw this first)

Draw their world before you draw yours. Use six zones. Fill each from their answers — write their words, not your labels. The goal is a shared, accurate picture of what exists today, warts included.

Zone A · Systems of record
What runs the business — Salesforce, SAP, ServiceNow, core banking, mainframe, key databases. These are fixed points; everything else connects to them.
Zone B · Integration & middleware today
How systems talk now — Anypoint? A competitor ESB? Point-to-point? iPaaS? Custom code? Batch files? Capture the mess honestly; it's your baseline.
Zone C · API estate & maturity
Do they have managed APIs, a catalog, a gateway? Is reuse happening or is everything bespoke? How discoverable are existing services? (This predicts how fast agents can be wired to real systems.)
Zone D · AI / agent initiatives in flight
Every agent, copilot, LLM app, or PoC already underway — team, stack, and whether it touches systems of record yet. Mark which are siloed.
Zone E · Ownership & governance
Who owns integration? Who owns APIs? Who owns "AI"? Is agent governance anyone's job yet? Draw the org lines — gaps here are pains later.
Zone F · Pains & constraints
Where it hurts today: fragile integrations, no discovery, security/compliance limits, cloud mandates, cost pressure, skills gaps. Circle the pains — you'll attach to-be boxes directly to them.

Discovery questions (to fill Canvas 1)

Ask, listen, write onto the zones. Each group feeds a zone. Don't move to the to-be until the as-is feels honest and complete.

Systems & integration (Zones A, B)

  • What are your core systems of record, and what connects them today?
  • What's your integration platform — Anypoint, something else, hand-rolled?
  • Where is integration most fragile or most expensive to change right now?

API estate (Zone C)

  • Do you have managed APIs and a catalog, or is most access bespoke?
  • If a new project needs customer data, do they find an API or build one?
  • Who governs API security, versioning, and access today?

AI / agent landscape (Zone D)

  • Where are you with agents — pilots, production, planning? On what stacks?
  • Are any agents calling systems of record yet? How was that wired?
  • Do different teams' agents need to cooperate, or are they siloed?

Ownership, governance & pain (Zones E, F)

  • Who owns integration, APIs, and "AI" — same team or three teams?
  • When an agent calls an API, who controls auth, rate limits, and scope?
  • If five teams build three agents each next year, how do you find and govern them all?
  • What has to be true for leadership to trust agents in production?

Canvas 2 — co-define the to-be (overlay onto the as-is)

Now draw the target — but anchor every box to a pain circled on Canvas 1. Draw on a second sheet beside the first so the overlay is visible: their systems stay; the fabric goes on top. Say the pain, then draw the box.

Draw 1 · Consuming agents (top)
Answers Zone D sprawl. "Your Agentforce and custom agents become governed consumers — different stacks, one fabric."
Draw 2 · Agent Broker
Answers "who decides which agent handles what". "Routes and delegates by intent, policy, identity — not hardcoded into every agent."
Draw 3 · Omni Gateway (wide bar)
Answers Zone E/F governance gap. "One control plane for every A2A and MCP hop — auth, rate-limit, observability. This is API Manager, for agents."
Draw 4 · MCP servers + specialist agents
Answers "how do agents reach our systems". "MCP Bridge turns an existing API into an agent tool with no code; the connectors do it in a Mule flow when you need logic."
Draw 5 · Anypoint APIs → their systems (reuse Canvas 1)
Answers Zone C. "This layer you may already own — draw the arrow down into the Canvas 1 systems. Agents ride on what you have; they don't replace it."
Draw 6 · Registry + Visualizer (down the side)
Answers discovery + visibility pains. "Agent Registry in Exchange for reuse; Visualizer + Monitoring so you can see who calls whom."

Name the gap & the path

Put both canvases side by side and make the delta explicit. This is where positioning becomes concrete — MuleSoft is the bridge across the gap, not a rip-and-replace.

DimensionAs-is (Canvas 1)To-be (Canvas 2)MuleSoft's role in the gap

Agent-to-agentDirect or none, ungovernedA2A through one gatewayOmni Gateway + A2A connector
Discovery / reuseTeams rebuild; no catalogRegistry in ExchangeAgent Registry extends existing Exchange
GovernanceNobody owns itCentral control plane + monitoringOmni Gateway policies + Visualizer
"Notice what's not changing: your systems of record and most of your APIs stay exactly where they are. What we're adding is the governed layer on top so agents can use what you already built — safely, and without every team reinventing it. The smallest first step is one existing API, exposed as an MCP tool with MCP Bridge — no code — so you can prove the pattern in days, not a quarter."

Test for the technical win (50–58 min)

A whiteboard that ends "great session!" but doesn't surface the decision mechanics is a nice chat, not a win. Actively probe these before you leave.

Surface the decision criteria
  • "What will your team measure to decide this is the right foundation?"
  • "Who else has to be convinced — security, architecture review, platform ownership?"
  • "Are you comparing approaches? What would the alternative look like on this same board?"
Know where MuleSoft genuinely wins (lead with these)
  • Reuse of existing estate — agents ride on APIs they already own; competitors often mean rebuild.
  • Ugly-backend reach — SAP, mainframe, SOAP: integration depth, not just an LLM gateway.
  • One governance plane for MCP and A2A — not a bolt-on per protocol.
  • Open protocols — bet on interoperability, not a single vendor's agent stack.
Red flags you do NOT have the win yet
  • They love the vision but can't name a system or use case to start with.
  • The technical evaluator stayed quiet — you sold the room, not the decider.
  • No agreed proof point or success metric — "let's keep talking" is not a criterion.
  • A competitor's platform is drawn in Zone B and nobody said how you displace or coexist.
What actually closes the technical win
A scoped, time-boxed proof against their as-is: take one real API from Canvas 1, expose it via MCP Bridge, call it from an agent through the gateway, show it governed in Monitoring. Small, real, on their systems. Agree the scope on the board before you leave.

Objections — quick responses

They sayYou say
"Our agents are just a pilot — this is overkill.""For one agent, skip it. Draw the picture now so at fifteen agents across five teams you're not retrofitting governance onto a mess. Cheap to plan, expensive to retrofit."
"Agentforce already does agents — why MuleSoft?""Agentforce is a consumer at the top of Canvas 2. The question is what it reaches and how it's governed. MuleSoft connects it to your SAP/mainframe and governs the traffic — different job, same picture."
"We already have an integration platform.""Then keep it in Zone B — the question is whether it governs agent-to-agent and agent-to-tool traffic and reaches your backends. Let's draw where it stops and where the gap is."
"We'll just let each team build and call directly.""That's the point-to-point API mess reborn one level up. Draw ten agents calling each other directly — that spaghetti is exactly what the fabric prevents."
"Isn't MCP/A2A too new to bet on?""Open protocols with fast-growing adoption — you're betting on interoperability, not a vendor. And the governance layer pays off regardless of which protocol wins."

Session outputs — artifacts that advance the win

  • Photo of Canvas 1 (as-is) — the shared, accurate picture of their world. This alone earns credibility.
  • Photo of Canvas 2 (to-be) — the target they co-authored, overlaid on their systems.
  • The gap table — as-is → to-be → MuleSoft's role, in their language.
  • An agreed proof scope — one real API, exposed and governed, as the technical-win close.
  • Named decision criteria + the evaluator — who signs off and what they measure.
  • A gap list — anything you flagged "let me verify"; follow-up builds trust in a technical eval.
  • Leave-behind: the reference architecture doc as the clean version of Canvas 2.
The habit that wins technical evals
Spend more time on Canvas 1 than feels comfortable. A to-be picture only positions MuleSoft if it's visibly built on their real landscape. The as-is capture is the credibility; the gap table is the argument; the proof scope is the close. The pretty diagram is the least important part.
