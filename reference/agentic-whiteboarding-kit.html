Whiteboarding kit — MuleSoft in the Agentic Enterprise

A run-a-session-cold field kit: a timed agenda, discovery questions, the architecture drawn box-by-box in the order you draw it live, the narrative, objection handling, and what to leave behind. Open it on your laptop five minutes before you walk into the room.

The whole session in one line
Don't present a finished diagram — build it with them. Start from their pain ("your agents are about to sprawl like your APIs did"), draw the layered fabric one box at a time as the answer to each pain, and end with a picture they co-authored. The goal isn't to show the architecture; it's to make them describe their world in its terms.
Before you quote product specifics
Agent Fabric, Omni Gateway, MCP Bridge, and the MCP/A2A connectors are new and fast-moving (several GA'd in 2026). Component roles and the architecture shape here are stable and safe to whiteboard. Exact feature lists, GA dates, and dialog details drift — verify current specifics before making commitments. On a whiteboard you're selling the shape, not the datasheet; if asked something you're unsure of, capture it as a follow-up rather than guessing. See the agentic reference architecture doc for the verified detail behind each box.

The 45-minute arc

Adjust to your slot, but keep the order: pain before picture. Never draw a box before they've felt the problem it solves.

0–5Frame & permission. "I don't want to present — I want to whiteboard your agent estate with you. Can I ask questions and sketch as we go?" Get the pen.
5–15Discovery. Current agent landscape + the sprawl pains. Write their words on the board — you'll attach boxes to them.
15–35Build the picture. Draw the fabric box-by-box (steps below), each as the answer to a pain they named.
35–42Trace a real request. Pick one of their use cases; walk it through the boxes. This is where it clicks.
42–45Capture & next step. Photo the board, agree one follow-up (deep-dive, PoC scope, or a gap to verify).

Pre-session prep checklist

  • Do they already own Anypoint? (If yes → this is "extend what you have," not "buy a new thing.")
  • What agent work is already happening — Agentforce? A data-science team with LangGraph? A copilot pilot?
  • Who's in the room — central IT / platform team, or a line-of-business AI team? (Changes the governance emphasis.)
  • Their messiest backends (SAP, mainframe, legacy SOAP)? Those are your strongest "why integration matters" anchors.
  • Any stated AI mandate from leadership (cost, risk, "agentic transformation")? Tie the picture to it.
  • Re-skim the reference architecture + MCP positioning docs so the detail is fresh.
  • Markers that work. Photo permission. Know your one follow-up ask.

Discovery questions (the 5–15 min block)

Ask, listen, write their answers on the board. You're mining for pains that map to boxes. Don't rush to draw.

Current agent landscape

  • Where are you with AI agents today — pilots, production, or planning?
  • Which teams are building them, and on what (Agentforce, a framework, in-house)?
  • Are any agents calling your systems of record yet? How did you wire that?
  • Do different teams' agents need to work together, or are they siloed today?

Sprawl & pain (the wedge)

  • If five teams each build three agents next year, how do you keep track of them?
  • How would one team discover an agent another team already built — or would they rebuild it?
  • When an agent calls an API, who controls auth, rate limits, and what it's allowed to touch?
  • If an agent misbehaves in production, can you see the chain of who called whom?

Integration estate

  • What do agents most need to reach — Salesforce, SAP, mainframe, databases?
  • Are those already fronted by APIs, or would each agent integrate from scratch?
  • Do you already have Anypoint / an API program? How mature is the catalog?

Governance & success

  • Who owns "agent governance" today — is it anyone's job yet?
  • What has to be true for leadership to trust agents in production?
  • Six months out, what does "this worked" look like for you specifically?

Build the picture — draw order

Draw bottom-up if they're integration-minded (start from systems they know), or top-down if they're AI-led (start from the agents they're building). Below is top-down. Each step names the pain it answers — say the pain, then draw the box.

Draw 1 · Consuming agents (top)
Pain: "everyone's building agents." Draw a row of boxes: Agentforce, a custom agent, a third-party agent. "These are your consumers. Different teams, different stacks — and that's fine, if they can cooperate and be governed."
Draw 2 · Agent Broker
Pain: "who decides which agent handles what?" One box below. "The broker routes and delegates work by intent, policy, and identity — so routing isn't hardcoded into every agent."
Draw 3 · Omni Gateway (the wide bar)
Pain: "auth, rate limits, visibility." Draw a full-width bar — nothing crosses without passing it. "Every hop — agent-to-agent (A2A) and agent-to-tool (MCP) — goes through one control plane. This is API Manager, for agents."
Draw 4 · MCP servers + specialist agents
Pain: "how do agents reach our systems?" Below the gateway, two branches: MCP servers (tools) and specialist agents. "MCP Bridge turns an existing API into an agent tool with no code; the MCP/A2A connectors do it inside a Mule flow when you need logic."
Draw 5 · Anypoint APIs → systems of record
Pain: "SAP, mainframe, the ugly stuff." Draw the API layer, then their actual systems. "This is the part you may already own. Agents ride on top of it — they don't replace it."
Draw 6 · Registry + Visualizer (down the side)
Pain: "discovery and seeing the mesh." Draw a vertical band spanning all layers. "The Agent Registry — in Exchange — is how teams find and reuse agents instead of rebuilding. The Visualizer + Monitoring show who calls whom."

The narrative (thread it through the draw)

"You lived through API sprawl — dozens of point-to-point integrations nobody could find or govern, and API-led connectivity fixed it. Agents are about to sprawl the exact same way, one level up: every team building them, on different stacks, calling each other directly, with no discovery and no control. This picture is the same play for agents. A registry so they're findable and reusable. A broker so work is routed by policy, not hardcoded. One gateway so every agent-to-agent and agent-to-tool call is authenticated and observable. And it speaks the open protocols — A2A between agents, MCP to tools — so your Agentforce, your custom agents, and vendor agents all live in one governed fabric. You don't rebuild for agents. You expose what you already have, governed."

Objections — quick responses

They sayYou say
"Our agents are just a pilot — this is overkill.""Totally — for one agent, skip all of it. Draw the picture now so that when it's fifteen agents across five teams, you're not retrofitting governance onto a mess. Cheap to plan, expensive to retrofit."
"Agentforce already does agents — why MuleSoft?""Agentforce is a consumer at the top of this board. The question is what it reaches and how it's governed. MuleSoft is the layer that connects agents to your SAP/mainframe/legacy and governs the traffic — different job, same picture."
"We'll just let each team build and call directly.""That's exactly the point-to-point API mess, reborn. Draw ten agents calling each other directly on the board — that spaghetti is the thing the fabric prevents."
"Isn't MCP/A2A too new to bet on?""They're open protocols with fast-growing adoption — betting on them is betting on interoperability, not a vendor. And the governance layer is valuable regardless of which protocol wins."
"We're all-in on one cloud's agent stack.""Great for building agents. This picture is about governing and connecting them to your estate — which is rarely all in one cloud. The gateway and connectors are the neutral ground."

Session outputs — leave with these

  • A photo of the co-authored board — with their pains written in their words next to the boxes.
  • One traced use case — a real request they care about, walked end-to-end through the layers.
  • A gap list — anything you flagged "let me verify" (don't fake it in the room — following up builds trust).
  • One agreed next step — a deeper architecture session, a scoped PoC (start with MCP Bridge on one existing API — fastest win), or a stakeholder readout.
  • Follow-up leave-behind: send the reference architecture doc as the "clean version" of what you drew together.
The one habit that makes these land
Draw slower than feels natural, and stop after every box to ask "does that match your world?" A whiteboard the customer co-authored is a commitment; a whiteboard you performed is a slide. The questions matter more than the drawing.
